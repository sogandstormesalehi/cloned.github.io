Abstract

Closed-loop neural stimulus optimisation finds images that maximally drive a target brain region using only scalar neural feedback. We build on surrogate-assisted genetic search over a structured prompt space. A core assumption shared across existing approaches is that the target region remains fixed throughout the session. In practice this is routinely violated: neural responses drift within minutes due to fatigue, habituation, and fluctuating attention, and the algorithm observes only a single blended scalar reward and is never told a shift has occurred. We study how to make surrogate-assisted search robust to this drift, simulating it by smoothly interpolating the reward across multiple fMRI-characterised brain regions so the algorithm must transition between targets blindly. Our central finding is that the benefit of state-awareness depends critically on the correlation structure between successive targets. When regions share image preferences, the incoming target rises before the transition as a side-effect of optimising the outgoing one, a free ride that stateless search exploits fully while state-aware methods disrupt it. When regions prefer opposing content, state-awareness wins by actively forgetting the pre-drift landscape. We introduce an adaptive window controller that infers the drift regime from the reward signal alone and adjusts the surrogate's memory accordingly, avoiding both failure modes without requiring any prior knowledge of the underlying cause for drift.

I. Introduction

The visual cortex is not a passive recorder but a highly selective system where different regions respond strongly to specific categories of content. The fusiform face area lights up for faces [Kanwisher et al. 1997], the parahippocampal place area for scenes [Epstein & Kanwisher 1998], and the extrastriate body area for bodies [Downing et al. 2001]. Understanding these selectivities, and generating images that drive them on demand, has broad applications: from probing the functional organisation of the brain to designing stimuli for neuroprosthetics and closed-loop therapeutic systems.

Methods such as BrainDiVE [Luo et al. 2023] and BrainACTIV [Cerdas et al. 2024] have shown that neural encoding models can guide generative models to synthesise maximally activating images by backpropagating through the encoder. This works well in silico but does not transfer directly to a real experimental session: actual neural responses from a human subject are not differentiable, and the encoding model is only an approximation of the true brain. A more realistic setting treats the oracle as a pure input-output function where the algorithm proposes images, the brain returns a scalar score, and no gradients are available. This is also expensive: a typical fMRI session yields only a few hundred usable image presentations before the subject fatigues, making sample efficiency critical.

A shared assumption across existing approaches is that the target brain region stays fixed throughout the session. In real sessions this is routinely violated: a subject's neural responses to the same image can shift meaningfully within minutes as fatigue accumulates, attention fluctuates, or habituation sets in [Grill-Spector et al. 2006]. This problem is most acute in EEG, where the signal is noisier and faster-changing than fMRI, making stable region-level targeting harder to maintain across a session. We use fMRI region activations as a controlled simulation substrate precisely because their selectivity is well-characterised, but the drift regimes we study are motivated by the messier reality of EEG deployment. A surrogate that has accumulated evidence about the pre-drift landscape will keep exploiting it even after the target has shifted, actively steering the search in the wrong direction, and the algorithm has no direct access to the shift, only a single blended scalar reward.

We build on prior work that uses surrogate-assisted genetic search over a structured text prompt space to find images that maximally drive target brain regions, and extend it with drift-aware components. Our central contribution is a study of when and why state-awareness helps. When the pre- and post-drift targets share image preferences, the incoming region was already rising before the transition as a side-effect of optimising the outgoing one, and state-awareness actively hurts here by suppressing content that was already working. When the regions prefer opposing content, state-awareness wins by forgetting the pre-drift landscape. This insight motivates an adaptive window controller that infers the drift regime from the reward signal alone and adjusts the surrogate's memory accordingly, without any prior knowledge of the target correlation structure.

II. Related Work

Neural Encoding Models and Closed-Loop Image Optimisation

Our algorithm treats the brain as a black-box oracle returning a scalar reward per image. In simulation this reward comes from a neural encoding model; in a real session it would come from neural recordings directly. The Natural Scenes Dataset [Allen et al. 2022] provides 7T fMRI responses for 8 subjects across tens of thousands of naturalistic images, and BERG builds pre-trained encoding models for 23 visual ROIs per subject, serving as our oracle. The idea of closed-loop neural stimulus optimisation originates in electrophysiology: XDream [Ponce et al. 2019] used a genetic algorithm to maximise single-neuron firing in macaque visual cortex, and NeuroGen [Gu et al. 2022] extended this to human fMRI with BigGAN. A shared assumption across these works is that the target region remains fixed throughout the session, which our work relaxes.

Surrogate-Assisted Evolutionary Search

Surrogate-assisted evolutionary algorithms pair a population-based search with a cheap proxy model that pre-screens offspring before each expensive oracle call, reducing oracle queries by orders of magnitude [Jones et al. 1998]; [He et al. 2023]. Maintaining surrogate quality when the objective landscape itself shifts has received comparatively little attention: existing methods are largely designed for static objectives, and the dynamic setting remains an open problem [Li & Chen 2022]. Standard methods accumulate all historical evaluations; under drift the growing pool of stale observations actively misleads the surrogate. Recency windowing is the natural fix, but setting the window length without prior knowledge of the drift structure is non-trivial. Our adaptive window controller addresses this by inferring the appropriate memory length from the reward trajectory alone.

Concept Drift Detection and Adaptation

Learning from shifting data streams is well-studied in the online learning literature [Gama et al. 2014]. Detection-based methods monitor error rates and trigger resets on degradation; window-based methods such as ADWIN [Bifet & Gavaldà 2007] maintain an adaptive sliding window with statistical guarantees. The foundational problem of concept drift, that models built on old data become inconsistent with new data as the underlying distribution shifts, was systematically characterised by [Tsymbal 2004], whose taxonomy of drift types informs our distinction between smooth and abrupt transitions. Our controller uses the slope-to-variance ratio of the recent reward trajectory as a detection signal to drive a forgetting-like mechanism, adapted to the specific setting of a genetic search with no access to ground-truth labels.

3.1 Search Space and Base Algorithm

The search space and base algorithm follow an approach developed in ongoing work in our lab. We search a structured text prompt space of approximately $10^{23}$ candidates, where each candidate is a tuple of indices across 17 semantic dimensions decoded into a natural language prompt and rendered by SDXL-Turbo. A BERG fMRI encoding model scores each rendered image, serving as the oracle. Because querying the oracle for every candidate is infeasible, we use a CLIP text surrogate, a Ridge ensemble trained on observed (prompt, score) pairs, to filter the search: 1000 candidates are bred and scored by the surrogate each generation, and only the top 20 by acquisition score are sent to the oracle.

Drift is simulated by blending the rewards of multiple ROIs using a chain of sigmoid transitions. For $N$ ROIs with $N-1$ transition midpoints $m_1 < m_2 < \cdots < m_{N-1}$, we define $s_i(t) = \sigma\!\left(k\!\left(\tfrac{t}{T} - m_i\right)\right)$ with $k = 6$ throughout, and assign weights \[ w_1(t) = 1 - s_1, \quad w_i(t) = s_{i-1}\,(1 - s_i) \text{ for } 1 < i < N, \quad w_N(t) = s_{N-1}. \] The blended reward is $r(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i(t)\,\tilde{r}_i$, where $\tilde{r}_i$ is the normalised score for ROI $i$. Transition midpoints are set to $m = 0.60$ for two-ROI sequences (200 evals), $(m_1, m_2) = (0.40, 0.70)$ for three-ROI sequences (300 evals), and $(m_1, m_2, m_3) = (0.33, 0.56, 0.78)$ for four-ROI sequences (300 evals). The individual ROI scores are never revealed; the algorithm observes only the blended scalar $r(t)$.

The two-ROI condition has a single transition at eval 120 (out of 200); the three-ROI condition transitions at evals 120 and 210 (out of 300); and the four-ROI condition transitions at evals 99, 168, and 234 (out of 300).

3.2 Recency Windowing

Without drift awareness, the surrogate accumulates the full session history and anchors the search to the pre-drift landscape. We address this by restricting surrogate training to the $W_s$ most recently evaluated prompts and elite selection to the $W_e$ most recent candidates, so that stale observations from an earlier target region do not dominate the surrogate's fit. Both windows are coupled so that the candidates used for breeding are always drawn from the same recency horizon as the surrogate's training data. $W_s$ is initialised to the full session length, making the algorithm effectively stateless at the start, and is subsequently controlled by the adaptive window controller described in Section 3.4.

3.3 State-Conditioned Surrogate

Recency windowing limits how much stale history the surrogate sees, but it cannot distinguish between a reward that is rising because the search is improving and one that is declining because the target has shifted. To give the surrogate access to this context, we augment each training point's CLIP text embedding with a three-dimensional session state descriptor: \[ \mathbf{s} = \bigl[\,\mu,\;\dot{r},\;\sigma^2\,\bigr] \] where $\mu$, $\dot{r}$, and $\sigma^2$ are the mean, slope, and variance of the blended reward over the most recent 20 evaluations at the time that training point was collected. The surrogate learns $\hat{r} = f(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{s})$, conditioning predictions on both image content and current session dynamics. At inference time, each candidate in the pool is augmented with the state descriptor at the current evaluation index before scoring.

3.4 Adaptive Window Controller

The recency window $W_s$ is controlled by a per-generation drift detector operating entirely on the blended reward signal. After each generation, the controller maintains a rolling buffer of the last $B = 5$ generation means and computes: \[ \text{drift_score} = \frac{-\,\text{slope}(\bar{g}_{t-B:t})} {\text{var}(\bar{g}_{t-B:t}) + \varepsilon} \] This quantity is large when the reward is declining quietly, negative slope with low variance, which is the characteristic signature of a high-correlation free ride ending. It remains low when the reward drops sharply and noisily, the anti-correlated case, which is handled by the slope trigger described below.

When the drift score exceeds $\tau = 0.2$, the window shrinks: $W_s \leftarrow \max(W_{\min},\; W_s \times 0.6)$, and an explorer injection is forced to introduce candidates from distant regions of the prompt space. When the score remains below zero for three consecutive generations, the window grows back: $W_s \leftarrow \min(W_{\max},\; W_s \times 1.15)$. The controller is initialised at $W_s = W_{\max} = 100$ and the floor is $W_{\min} = 20$.

A secondary slope trigger fires when the linear slope over the last 20 evaluations falls below $-0.005$, injecting candidates from the most distant CLIP-PCA clusters relative to already-evaluated prompts. The two mechanisms are complementary: the window controller handles smooth correlated drift by adjusting surrogate memory, while the slope trigger handles sharp stagnation by diversifying the candidate pool.

IV. Experiments

4.1 Setup

We evaluate across three conditions of increasing complexity: eight two-ROI pairs (200 evals, one transition at eval 120), eight three-ROI triplets (300 evals, transitions at evals 120 and 210), and four four-ROI quadruplets (300 evals, transitions at evals 99, 168, and 234). All conditions use 20 random seeds. We compare three algorithms: Stateless (SL; no windowing, full history), Classic state-aware (SA; fixed $W_s = 40$, $W_e = 20$, state descriptor, slope trigger), and Adaptive state-aware (AD; $W_s$ initialised at 100 and controller-driven, state descriptor, slope trigger). Component ablations were conducted to isolate individual contributions.

Our primary metric is peak new ROI score: mean raw activation of the new target region over the last 50 evaluations. We additionally report adaptation quality ($\Delta Q$): the simultaneous rise of the new region and fall of the old over a $\pm 50$-eval window around the transition: \[ \Delta Q = \bigl(\overline{\text{raw}_b}^{\,\text{post}} - \overline{\text{raw}_b}^{\,\text{pre}}\bigr) - \bigl(\overline{\text{raw}_a}^{\,\text{post}} - \overline{\text{raw}_a}^{\,\text{pre}}\bigr) \] and crossover: whether the new region's 10-eval rolling mean ever exceeded the old region's. All comparisons are Cohen's $d$ relative to stateless, assessed by Mann-Whitney U test.

As a sanity check, we also evaluate all three algorithms on static single-target optimisation (no drift) across six visual ROIs. SA consistently underperforms SL in the final-window score across all ROIs, while AD approximately matches SL. This is expected: without drift, the recency windowing and state-tracking machinery in SA actively waste evaluations on candidates that are informative about session dynamics rather than the target itself. AD avoids this by expanding its window when no drift signal is detected, effectively recovering stateless behaviour. This confirms that the adaptive controller's core property — doing nothing when there is nothing to adapt to — holds even in the simplest possible regime.

Table 4 shows the clustering does not beat the SW baseline: Every explorer variant lands within noise of it (peak PPA 0.542–0.546 vs 0.555; $d_{\text{SW}} = -0.10$ to $-0.35$, all n.s.). As the surrogate is windowed and state-conditioned, the population recovers from its own breeding fast enough that the injection-time choice of prompts stops mattering. One suspicion was that our clusters live in CLIP, not BERG: prompts far apart in CLIP can still trigger near-identical PPA activations, so "diverse in CLIP" is not "diverse for the oracle". Yet clustering on BERG features directly (${\sim}10,000$ prompts) was significantly worse, hence the embedding space wasn't the problem.

VI. Conclusion and Limitations

How do you optimize for a brain region that keeps changing, when all you ever see is a single number? Our answer is: by learning to forget selectively. We extended surrogate-assisted neural image search with drift-aware components and showed that the benefit of state-awareness depends critically on the correlation structure between successive target regions. On anti-correlated and near-zero pairs, state-aware methods win decisively by actively forgetting the pre-drift landscape. On highly correlated pairs, they hurt, suppressing a region that was already rising for free. The adaptive window controller resolves this tension by inferring the regime from the blended reward alone, matching stateless performance where the free ride is available while recovering the state-aware advantage where genuine drift must be tracked. In multi-region sequences, the advantage compounds over the session: by the second and third transition, both state-aware methods significantly outperform stateless, and the adaptive controller's margin widens as it has had more time to converge within each regime.

Limitations. Drift in our experiments is simulated via a smooth sigmoid schedule. Rapid or oscillating drift would be unreliable to test in simulation as the search space is large enough that the surrogate cannot track a target shifting faster than it can converge, and faithfully reproducing real neural drift requires real recordings. That said, our evaluation spans a broad range of ROI pairs with varying correlation structure, from strongly anti-correlated to highly correlated, providing meaningful coverage of the regimes a real system would encounter. Most importantly, the method has not yet been validated on real neural recordings; our most aggressive drift conditions are intended to approximate the faster, noisier regime of EEG, but true validation will require moving beyond simulated BERG scores to live recordings.
